Problem Solving Framework & Principles for Software Engineers

As I am reaching the 10-years-of-experience milestone in my career, there‚Äôs still a bunch of stuff that I am hopeless at. However, what mostly keeps my impostor syndrome at bay is a couple of things that I have understood well.

For example, testing eluded me a decade ago when I started my professional journey as a junior software engineer. Writing code that will exercise and check my code for correctness just blew my mind. Truthfully, it still blows my mind, but for entirely different reasons.

Developing my approach to work has been another eluding thing. For a good part of my career, I have struggled to figure out how to approach projects. So often, folks think that just by having the experience, they will develop a framework to approach their work, but they‚Äôre usually wrong. I‚Äôve learned that hard skills and abilities are useless without a tactical approach to problem-solving.

In continuation, I will share my story of how I built a problem-solving framework and how you can also use its tenets and tactics to aid you in your everyday work.

Misconceptions #

In the first few years of my career, I didn‚Äôt think much about ‚Äúan approach‚Äù to my work. I was trying to stay afloat. My leaders would assign me tickets with feature/change requests, and after I understood what was needed and why I would be off to the races. Write the code, open a pull request, get a review, and (hopefully) merge it. Sprint by sprint, rinse, and repeat.

Early on, I realized that folks around me were productive and consistently delivered high quality and (mostly) on time. Which was great ‚Äì I was in good company to learn. I always wrote that off to their experience, talent, intelligence, education, or any combination.

As time progressed, I saw less experienced folks being very productive and producing excellent code. Collaboration with them was a breeze, with a good understanding of the domain, asking thoughtful questions, and creating good code, at least by my standards.

As much as these were intelligent folks, it was not raw intelligence that made them efficient ‚Äì not that it didn‚Äôt help them ‚Äì but I found out soon enough that they had developed their way of approaching problems. Finding this out was the reflection point for me, where I realized that to get to the next level, I need a problem-solving framework that I can reuse in my everyday work.

Problem clarity #

Can you imagine getting a simple math assignment at school such as ‚Äú3 - 2 * 5‚Äù without familiarizing what each sign means or their order of precedence? That would be the equivalent of starting to code right out of the gate when presented with a problem. Yes, even if it‚Äôs just a bit of prototype code that you will throw away (you hope!).

Having an understanding of the problem that you are solving is as important as the solution itself. By spending time to get clarity, you will save time building the solution. Unfortunately, I have been guilty of doing the opposite early in my career ‚Äì jumping to prototyping and making assumptions about the problem.

If you are a builder at heart, it‚Äôs normal to be excited and jump on the code. And I appreciate it when I see the enthusiasm to produce an artifact of your work. But I soon found out that there‚Äôs a way to get both: the dopamine hit from building without having to jump the gun and begin coding..

It‚Äôs called sketching.

Sketching to the rescue #

These days, when presented with a problem, I take a simple step: open a blank document. I usually use Dropbox Paper at work and Google docs for personal writing (such as this). I put the problem statement at the top, jotting down everything I know about the problem. Then, I link various supporting resources: code in version control, (pseudo-)code snippets, documentation, examples of similar issues, etc.

In this context, I define ‚Äúsketching‚Äù as explaining the problem that I am about to begin solving through writing to organize my thoughts.

I think sketching turns into magic when I write about the problem and not the solution. It‚Äôs like my thoughts stare back at me. They force me to read them and assess my reasoning of the problem. So **sketching is introspection on how well I understand the problem. **And as we discussed earlier, no problem is solvable without first understanding its ins and outs.

Also, I think about the second-order effects of the problem. For example, if I dig into what new sales report the users asked for, I like to think about who else will use the new report beyond the user. The accountants of the users, perhaps? Or an auditor? I branch off and explore, allowing me to understand the problem from different angles better.

Cross-pollinate #

When I reach a point where I am happy with my sketch of the problem, once again, I get the urge to jump on the solution and code away. The improved clarity of the problem that I now have gives me more confidence (as it should!), but I still think it’s not good to start with coding. Granted, it wouldn’t be ideal if I jumped on a prototype at this point, but it would be better than before.

With such writing exercises, it’s too easy to go too deep or wide, so it’s a good practice to get calibrated by others. The peer feedback aids with prioritization, as it allows me to focus on the right thing within the current time/resource constraints. Some aspects of the problem will have higher priority/impact in the short term, and other elements, while still relevant, can be addressed later.

Once I have a draft problem exploration document, the best course of action is to share it with a group of folks that are familiar with the problem space. I still don’t know the solution, so I prefer not to talk to folks close to potential solution space. My goal is to share my understanding of the problem and get feedback on it, not to get my problem solved.

Use the hivemind #

Many folks struggle at this point ‚Äì exposing one’s understanding of a problem they will be solving can make one feel vulnerable. I have had impostor-like feelings when I am supposed to share my work, which has held me back from sharing many times. So many questions have popped up in my mind, like “what if my boss realizes I know nothing about the problem?” or “what if they realize I am a fraud?”.

I don’t have a pill to get rid of those thoughts, but a perspective that I try to keep: the only way to eliminate my ignorance is by exposing it. And the best audience to do that is my closest allies, the folks I work with every day. They are the hive mind that I use to surface any ignorance on the problem space and allow them to poke holes in it.

My approach has simplified over the years: I say something like, “Hey, I put down some thoughts on the project we are kicking off; I’d appreciate it if you can check it out when you have X minutes”.

Embrace feedback #

I find writing very revealing: my thoughts on “paper” for the world to consume. Therefore it’s hard when I get feedback that I don’t like - it is fair, but it stings. When receiving unfavorable feedback, my go-to action was to defend my writing. Still, lately, I’ve tried something drastically different: I critique my writing too and dig deeper into others' feedback.

Defending your work is typical and frankly expected. But my intention in sharing the sketch is to get feedback on what I got wrong in it or find the blind spots that I have. The whole reasoning behind sketching the problem’s depth and breadth was to surface my misunderstanding and eliminate it.

Hence, I always try to embrace the feedback on the sketch, ask “why” back to the reviewers so I can learn more, and use their input to build my critique of the writing further.

Iterate #

For one to decide on an iterative approach, they have to be at peace that their work won’t be great in the first few attempts. Like building software, where people have realized that delivering software as one big chunk is dangerous and expensive as there’s not enough room and time to fold in feedback, the beauty of iterative work is that it shortens the feedback loop.

I try to keep the same principle when sketching out problems: it’s better to have a half-baked sketch done now, share it with others, and learn from their feedback instead of obsessing over details and losing the opportunity to improve.

From what I’ve seen in the wild, folks that are earlier in their career tend to overcomplicate their life by trying to get a polished writeup before sharing. I consider it an anti-pattern ‚Äì I prefer to share a quick draft and receive some harsh feedback rather than spend days on a sketch before another pair of eyes reads it.

A good take on this is the one from Facebook’s early Little Red Book:

Fast is better than slow. While slow is adding unnecessary embellishments, fast is out in the world. And that means that fast can learn from experience while slow can only theorize.

Prototype #

In line with the same “fast is better than slow” mantra, I try not to dwell on the problem for too long. Instead, I decide that I understand the problem enough to move on to the prototype. When I begin prototyping, I try to be very cautious because solving the wrong or wrongly shaped problem is very costly.

Jay Shah, one of the reviewers of this essay, asked me, “what is a good indicator that you’re ready to prototype?” ‚Äì a question I loved. Of course, there’s always the risk of not knowing enough to begin prototyping, but there are tactics to de-risk that. To start prototyping, a two-part indicator I use is:

  1. I shared my problem sketch with multiple people who have a vested interest in the team solving the right problem, e.g., tech lead, product manager, engineering manager, and other engineers, depending on the organization’s layout and size.
  2. I have addressed all blocking feedback from the reviewers; I can address nits or tangential commentary during implementation (or never).

Another tactic that I can offer that I tend to use if I want to be extra-careful:** if my sketch of the prototype is sound, I am ready to begin prototyping.**

Sketching the prototype #

At this point, I try to think of the solution space for the first time, so I finally get to scratch that itch. A prototype sketch does not have to be very detailed ‚Äì I try to keep sketches concise with just enough detail to show the nitty-gritty of the implementation. I think about where the rubber meets the road in the project I am working on, and I will try to sketch out only that part.

For example, if I am to implement a new sales report, I will sketch out three aspects of it:

  • Sketch the module that generates the report: preliminary name, entry interface, dependencies, main algorithm (e.g., loops over each sale, extracts amounts, dumps them in a dictionary, etc.)
  • Sketch the integration of the module with the rest of the product, e.g., how and where the integration invokes the new module
  • (If a front-end component is involved) Sketch of plumbing between the front-end and the back-end

Admittedly, my perspective is skewed towards a backend-first approach, as that’s where my experience lies, but I think the principles apply to other areas too.

Solicit feedback #

Given that I usually build the prototype with other engineers, this time, getting feedback from them is sufficient. Sometimes getting a review from a neighboring team is also good, especially if we end up changing parts of the codebase they own. However, if the changes are more extensive, like changing an API owned by another team, I tend to consider their feedback blocking ‚Äì no approval means no further progress.

Once I gather feedback from all parties, I am ready to proceed with prototyping if there’s no blocking feedback. When a teammate or someone external leaves blocking feedback that’s not easy to resolve, I have found that the best course of action is to get on a call or sit next to each other (remember those times?) and work through it. Getting feedback just over text can feel distant and inhumane, so it’s best to discuss and collaborate so both parties can walk away feeling like winners.

Prototype away #

Once I reach this part, I feel that my approach is sound, realistic, and cohesive with the codebase I will build in. I continue to prototype my way into the problem, dealing with every aspect of the problem in the current scope (remember: some parts of the problem might be out of scope, as discussed earlier).

I tend to keep my prototypes short but holistic, and I tend not to over-engineer. The least amount of code that I can write to prove that the approach will work ‚Äì the better. Basically, prototyping meets code-golf.

Once I am confident of the feasibility and the approach, I usually throw away the code and work with other engineers/the team to build out the first iteration of a well designed, documented, tested, and deployable version of the solution.

Principles #

If I sublimated my whole approach to exploring, sketching, and solving a problem, I would put it as follows:

  1. Strive for problem clarity: you can‚Äôt produce an excellent solution to a problem you don‚Äôt understand well
  2. Write, write, write: writing is a thinking tool ‚Äì use it!
  3. Sketch early and often: it‚Äôs much cheaper to get calibrated on your one-pager outlining your perception/thinking instead of on your first pull request.
  4. Use the hivemind: ask for feedback, learn what you don‚Äôt know, get comfortable with the uncomfortable and steal ideas
  5. Iterate: only Bob Ross gets it perfect on the first try; everyone else takes a lot of shots before they nail it.
